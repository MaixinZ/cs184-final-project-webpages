  <section>
    <article className="paper-card">
      <span className="paper-label">Problem Description</span>
      <h2>What technical problem we want to solve</h2>
      <div className="copy-block">
        Naive full-screen stylization is easy to apply, but it often fails
        unevenly on dense urban scenes. In <em>Cyberpunk 2077</em>, emissive
        signage, distant skyline structure, reflective surfaces, HUD or UI
        overlays, and dense foreground geometry all respond differently to the
        same post-process effect. A single global filter can therefore blow out
        neon highlights, flatten background structure, wash out local detail, or
        contaminate overlays that should remain legible.
      </div>
      <div className="copy-block">
        Our project studies whether a post-process pipeline can produce more
        coherent real-time stylization without assuming deep access to the game
        engine. The main technical challenge is to build several usable
        full-scene styles, then control them in a region-aware way so different
        parts of the same frame can be modulated differently and even
        juxtaposed through split-world composition. This is not full semantic
        segmentation and it is not a deep-learning-based scene parser. Instead,
        we will rely on region-aware stylization control driven by screen-space
        cues and shader heuristics.
      </div>
      <div className="copy-block">
        The control signals we expect to use include depth when available and
        stable, luminance or emissive intensity, edges and structure,
        foreground-versus-background separation, derived normals or edge proxies
        where feasible, hue or saturation or color-cluster masks, and local
        variance or texture-complexity masks. These heuristics should help us
        differentiate bright neon-heavy areas, skyline or background regions,
        dense detailed foreground regions, flatter low-detail surfaces, and
        highly emissive or high-contrast areas. The goal is not exact
        object-level understanding, but more coherent region-aware masking and
        modulation than a uniform full-screen pass.
      </div>
      <ul className="stack-list">
        <li>
          Can region-aware stylization improve coherence over naive uniform
          post-processing in Cyberpunk 2077 scenes?
        </li>
        <li>
          Can split-world composition make style differences more legible and
          artistically compelling within the same frame?
        </li>
      </ul>
    </article>
  </section>

      <h2>What we plan to deliver</h2>
      <div className="copy-block">
        Our baseline target by the final project is four usable scene-level
        styles inside <em>Cyberpunk 2077</em>. The required polished demo is
        narrower: we want at least two of the strongest styles shown on the
        same matched scene, including a split-world comparison and a temporal
        transition. All four styles will still be documented somewhere in the
        final website or video, even if final polish is concentrated on the
        strongest two.
      </div>
      <ul className="stack-list">
        <li>
          <strong>Multiple scene-level styles</strong>
          Build four usable full-scene styles in the Cyberpunk ReShade pipeline,
          with distinct visual behavior rather than minor preset changes.
        </li>
        <li>
          <strong>Split-world composition</strong>
          Implement a same-frame composition mode that uses line-based or
          shape-based masks so one side or region of the screen can use one
          style while another region uses a different style. This is a single
          composed frame, not multiple windows or multiple game instances.
        </li>
        <li>
          <strong>Region-aware stylization heuristics</strong>
          Modulate style strength with screen-space cues and heuristics instead
          of applying every effect uniformly across the full frame.
        </li>
        <li>
          <strong>Temporal transitions</strong>
          Implement at least one smooth style-to-style transition over time,
          such as moving split masks, and evaluate whether the result stays
          visually stable during camera motion.
        </li>
        <li>
          <strong>Matched-scene evaluation</strong>
          Record fixed-scene comparisons so we can compare vanilla rendering,
          naive stylization, region-aware stylization, and split-world output.
        </li>
      </ul>
    </article>

  <section className="page-grid">
    <article className="paper-card paper-card--accent">
      <span className="paper-label">Technical Risks</span>
      <h3>Main limitations and mitigation</h3>
      <ul className="stack-list">
        <li>
          <strong>Post-process and depth-buffer limitations</strong>
          We operate in a post-process regime rather than inside the game
          renderer. Depth access may be available but unstable or
          setting-dependent, and multiple depth buffers or clear operations may
          complicate depth-based effects. Mitigation: validate depth feasibility
          early, use ReShade depth debugging tools where appropriate, scope
          effects to scenes where depth is reliable, and keep fallback heuristics
          that depend less on fragile signals.
        </li>
        <li>
          <strong>Lack of exact object or material IDs</strong>
          Region-aware control will rely on screen-space signals and heuristics
          rather than exact semantic segmentation or full material-aware access.
          This limits fine-grained per-object control. Mitigation: focus on
          robust region-level effects, use artistic masks and split-world
          controls, and prioritize coherence over exact semantic correctness.
        </li>
        <li>
          <strong>Temporal instability and flicker</strong>
          Cyberpunk scenes contain dense geometry, strong emissives,
          reflections, moving objects, and complex lighting, so threshold-based
          logic may flicker during camera movement or style transitions.
          Mitigation: prefer soft transitions over hard thresholds, use
          temporally smoother masks where possible, benchmark on controlled
          camera paths, and focus first on stable low-frequency region logic.
        </li>
        <li>
          <strong>Scope risk from optional branches</strong>
          The 3DGS and Godot branches are meaningful but non-essential
          extensions. The main deliverable is the Cyberpunk stylization system
          itself. Mitigation: keep a strict mainline-first schedule and only
          pursue optional branches after the Cyberpunk pipeline is stable.
        </li>
      </ul>
